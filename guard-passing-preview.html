Week 1 — Grip Fighting & Foundation Passes

Before you pass the guard, you need to control the fight. My grip fighting system starts before any hand contact. Here's how I approach it.

Grip Fighting Hierarchy

First Contact — Establishing Dominance

1. Lapel Control: When I step in, my first move is always the lapel. I grab the far-side lapel at the collar bone, thumb in. This controls the elbow and limits their ability to frame or shrimp effectively.

2. Sleeve Control: My second hand grabs the same-side sleeve at the wrist. Thumb in, fingers wrapped. This removes their ability to grip my collar and prevents immediate spider or De La Riva entries.

3. Hip Shift: I step my lead foot to the outside of their hip, not the mat. This opens their guard immediately. Common mistake: stepping too close to the hips kills your angle.

Drill 1: The Knee Slice Pass

My Highest-Percentage Competition Pass

Setup: Opponent in closed guard. I control lapel and sleeve. Step to the side. Drop my shoulder into their chest, knee into their hip crease. Head to the opposite shoulder.

  • Keep heavy chest pressure throughout — never let up
  • Knee stays glued to their hip crease, not sliding up the thigh
  • Walk your body over theirs, don't lean back
  • Clear the head by posting on the mat with your free hand

Common Mistakes

  • Leaning back instead of forward
  • Letting their knee shield break the angle
  • Not clearing the head before trying to step over

Drill 2: Torreando (Over/Under Pass)

Most Underrated Pass in Gi BJJ

Opponent has a strong collar grip? Torreando is your answer. Both hands control their sleeves. Step your head to one side, hips to the other. They have to release one grip to frame. You read which grip releases and attack that side immediately.

  • Your head goes OPPOSITE to your hips — commit to the angle
  • Control both sleeves before you commit to the pass
  • Anticipate the grip release and pounce on it instantly
  • Once past the knees, pin their arm and clear the head

Drill 3: Double Under Pass

Standing Pass Option — Critical in Competition

My go-to when they have a lazy open guard. Both hands under their thighs, both wrists crossed. Stand up tall, control the hips, step around. This pass gives you a standing pass option which scores big in IBJJF rules.

  • Keep wrists crossed — never let one hand get isolated
  • Stand tall throughout — don't lean forward at all
  • Lead with the shoulder, not the hip
  • Step wide around the knee to avoid getting stuck
